Parent Guide

Practical Steps to Protect Your Child

Every parent needs a plan. This guide gives you that plan — the signs to watch for, how to talk without shaming, immediate next steps if you suspect use, and how to find help. Keep this guide on hand and share it with other parents in your community.

1. Understand the Risk

Drug use rarely looks like the movies. It usually starts as experimentation, mood changes, or secretiveness. Early recognition prevents escalation.

Key behaviors to watch for:

  • Sudden withdrawal or secrecy
  • Decline in grades or attendance
  • Unexplained money loss
  • New or suspicious friends
  • Changes in sleep or appetite
  • Unexplained injuries

2. What to Do If You Suspect Something

Stay calm — panic pushes kids away.

  • Gather facts discreetly — preserve dignity and privacy
  • Avoid immediate accusations — open with curiosity, not judgment
  • Choose the right time — private, calm, and when neither of you are emotional
  • Use an "I" statement — "I've noticed X and I'm worried because I love you."
  • Ask open questions — "Help me understand what's been going on."
  • Focus on safety — ask if they are in immediate danger or at risk of overdose

Immediate Safety Actions

If overdose is suspected — call emergency services now. Do not hesitate.

  • Secure any substances and paraphernalia where possible without compromising safety
  • Keep lines of communication open — let them know help is available and non-punitive

3. How to Run a Supportive Conversation

  • Use empathy, not lectures
  • Validate feelings: "I can see this is hard."
  • Avoid shaming language or blaming; this reduces help-seeking
  • Establish small, concrete steps together — e.g., meet a counselor, sleep routine, try one therapy appointment

4. Where to Get Help

Short-term:

  • Local helpline
  • Trusted school counselor
  • Emergency room

Long-term:

  • Medical evaluation (doctor/psychiatrist) for substance use disorder
  • Evidence-based rehab centers
  • Family counseling
  • Peer support groups

5. What Rehabilitation Looks Like

  • Detox = medical supervision for initial withdrawal if needed
  • Counseling = individual and family therapy follow-up
  • Aftercare = relapse prevention, peer groups, vocational support
  • Recovery is a process; relapse is possible but not failure

6. How to Protect Privacy and Legal Rights

  • You have the right to seek help without criminalizing the child in most family-first models
  • Always ask the care provider about confidentiality policies and what information will be shared with authorities

7. Practical Checklist (One Page, Printable)

  • Observe & note dates of concerning behavior
  • Have a calm conversation plan
  • Create a safety plan (phones, emergency contacts)
  • Book medical/mental health assessment
  • Connect with local support & follow up weekly

8. Community Actions Parents Can Take

  • Start or join parent groups
  • Request school workshops
  • Circulate verified helpline info across WhatsApp groups and local community boards

Closing & Key Message

You are not alone. Action, taken early and calmly, changes outcomes. Keep seeking help, keep talking, and keep showing up.
